Nonprofit humanitarian infrastructure

Community hubs that serve every day—and stand ready when disaster hits.

Project Beehive, by The Good Project

We build disaster-response community hubs called Beehives. Each one combines daily commercial activity with emergency preparedness: a thrift and outdoor gear store, a training center, and an emergency supply warehouse on ordinary days—and converts to shelter and local crisis command when the worst happens.

Our mission is to reduce human suffering through local, disaster-ready institutions and faster relief than traditional aid systems allow—starting in Tornado Alley, with a model designed to replicate.

Mission

Prepared neighbors. Faster response.

We help the individuals and families that make up a community become more prepared and resilient when crises arrive—by putting infrastructure, training, and supplies in place before they are needed.

Problem

Funding exists—but delivery is too slow.

Disaster funding is real, yet communities still wait. Too often, local areas lack pre-positioned infrastructure for an immediate response. In Tornado Alley, disasters are not rare—they are part of life.

  • We need more organizations that actively prepare communities before events occur.
  • We need more organizations that train people to deploy effectively in emergencies.
  • We need more organizations that store resources locally so help does not have to travel hundreds of miles first.

The Beehive model

Retail, training, warehouse—and a command posture for crisis.

A Beehive is a community preparedness hub: the market sells donated and sourced preparedness supplies to the public; the Storehouse holds rotating emergency stock; Disaster School runs paid preparedness classes and certifications year-round; the Command Center runs operations and coordinates relief.

Provisions

Emergency supply warehouse and retail—donated items, consignment, food donations, wholesale pricing, and pre-built emergency packs.

Disaster Team

Disaster-response training center: physical training, certifications, and ongoing membership for individuals and organizations.

Shelter & command

Built-in shelter conversion and a local crisis command center so the hub can coordinate response when events overwhelm normal capacity.

Revenue model

Built to reduce dependence on one-off fundraising.

The design combines earned revenue with endowment income so the work can scale without perpetual donor-only dependency.

  • Preparedness retail — sales of donated goods, consignment, food programs, wholesale relationships, and emergency packs.
  • Disaster School — membership and course fees for physical training and certifications.
  • Endowment yield — invested capital producing recurring income for long-term stability.

First Beehive

Springfield region, Missouri—proof of concept in Tornado Alley.

The first node anchors the network in a region where preparedness is not theoretical. Reif Tauati serves as founding operating president. Estimated one-time setup is approximately $4.5M, including building purchase, retrofit, and infrastructure upgrades. The operating case is modeled for the Springfield metro; the first facility targets the Ozark area as a central test bed for the Beehive playbook.

We start now—gathering donations and working from wherever we can—while pursuing capital for the flagship facility.

Capital plan

Initial raise: $20M

Proceeds are intended to fund the first Beehive, build a core endowment (~$8M target), provide operating runway, and reserve capital for a second node. The endowment is designed to generate recurring yield (modeled around a blended 5–6% net strategy), strengthening balance-sheet resilience. Yields are not legally guaranteed; they reflect a documented, actively managed approach rather than passive public-market assumptions.

Illustrative Year 1 economics for the Springfield case (from our planning materials): roughly $1.05M combined revenue (Provisions and Disaster School) against approximately $592K operating cost—before broader network and capital structure effects. Each future node is modeled with the same framework, adjusted for local population, real estate, and market density.

Expansion

A self-replicating regional network

After the Springfield-area flagship, the roadmap includes nodes such as Branson, Joplin, Bentonville, Tulsa, Kansas City, and St. Louis—each within a few hours’ drive of neighbors so the region can mobilize quickly. Beehives are designed so success at one node helps fund the next.

Governance

Local leadership with clear operating lanes

Each Beehive runs through PORTALS, adapted from high-discipline operating companies: People, Operations, Revenue, Treasury, Automations, Leads, and Story. Board composition includes the president and spouse and two counselors with spouses, alongside a local leadership council.

Who this is for

Philanthropic partners who build institutions

We are engaging private foundations, donor-advised funds, philanthropic high-net-worth individuals, and institutional charitable capital that wants durable infrastructure—not only event-based grants.

Timeline & stage

Pre-launch; execution begins with capital.

Once funded, launch of the first fully built Beehive is estimated at roughly 12–18 months. We prefer purchasing warehouses rather than leasing because of heavy retrofit requirements. No operational Beehive exists yet; this site reflects the pre-launch stage and active partnership building.
